### Root Causes of the Deficit

The $10.4 million gap does not have a single cause. It is the product of at least six converging forces:

#### 1. Declining Enrollment

Springfield's Kâ€“12 enrollment has dropped by approximately **800â€“1,000 students** over the past five years (as of the most recent available data). Oregon's State School Fund is distributed on a per-pupil basis through the ADMw (Average Daily Membership, weighted) formula. Fewer students = fewer dollars.

Key enrollment pressures include:
- Declining birth rates in Lane County
- Housing affordability driving families out of the district
- Growth of charter schools and home schooling post-COVID
- Competition from neighboring districts (Eugene 4J, Bethel)

Each lost student represents approximately **$10,000â€“$11,000** in State School Fund revenue at current funding levels.

#### 2. Flat State Funding Per-Pupil

Oregon's State School Fund â€” the single largest source of revenue for Springfield â€” has not kept pace with inflation. While the Legislature has increased the total fund in recent biennia, those increases have been consumed by:
- PERS cost shifts from the state to districts
- Enrollment growth in other parts of the state (Portland suburbs, Central Oregon)
- Inflation that outpaces nominal funding increases

In real (inflation-adjusted) dollars, Oregon per-pupil funding remains **below 2008 pre-recession levels** â€” a fact that shapes every budget conversation.

#### 3. The ESSER Cliff

The Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief (ESSER) funds â€” federal COVID-era dollars â€” expired on **September 30, 2024**. Springfield received approximately **$30â€“35 million** in total ESSER allocations across three rounds (ESSER I, II, and III). While the district was careful not to use ESSER for permanent staffing, some positions and programs supported by those funds have not been fully replaced by ongoing revenue.

The three rounds of ESSER funding had different timelines and spending requirements:

| Round | Approximate Amount | Deadline | Primary Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| ESSER I (CARES Act) | ~$4â€“5M | Sept 2022 | PPE, sanitization, devices, emergency staffing |
| ESSER II (CRRSA) | ~$10â€“12M | Sept 2023 | Learning loss, ventilation, mental health |
| ESSER III (ARP) | ~$18â€“22M | Sept 2024 | Extended learning, tutoring, summer programs, staffing |

Programs at risk include:
- Summer learning and extended-year programs
- Additional counselors and mental health staff hired during the pandemic
- Tutoring and intervention programs â€” particularly the high-dosage tutoring models shown to be effective in recovering pandemic learning loss
- Technology refresh cycles accelerated with ESSER dollars
- Supplemental curriculum materials and intervention resources
- Additional custodial and facilities improvements that maintained healthier learning environments

The ESSER cliff is particularly painful because many of these programs were **demonstrably effective**. Cutting them is not a reflection of their value â€” it is a reflection of the district's inability to sustain one-time federal funding levels with ongoing state and local revenue.

#### 4. Rising Personnel Costs

Personnel costs (salaries + benefits) account for approximately **85%** of the general fund budget. Three specific cost drivers are accelerating:

- **PERS (Public Employees Retirement System) employer rates:** Springfield's blended PERS rate has climbed from approximately 25% of payroll five years ago to over **30%** currently, with further increases projected. (See the PERS section below for detail.)
- **Health insurance:** The district's contribution to employee health insurance has increased at 6â€“10% annually in recent years â€” far outpacing revenue growth.
- **Step and column increases:** Under existing collective bargaining agreements, most employees receive annual step increases (longevity) and may also receive column increases (education credits). These "cost of continuing current operations" increases add roughly **2â€“3%** to the salary base each year even without any new negotiated raises.

#### 5. Inflation on Non-Personnel Costs

Energy, fuel, food service supplies, instructional materials, and contracted services have all experienced significant inflation since 2021. While non-personnel costs are only ~15% of the budget, even a 5â€“8% annual increase on a $20 million base adds over $1 million per year.

#### 6. Deferred Maintenance and Operational Pressures

Years of tight budgets have pushed maintenance, technology replacement, and curriculum adoption cycles behind schedule. While these are often funded from other sources, the general fund increasingly absorbs costs that cannot wait.

### Oregon School Funding Context

Understanding Springfield's deficit requires understanding how Oregon funds schools.

#### The State School Fund (SSF)

Oregon uses a **formula-based funding model** established by Measure 5 (1990) and the school funding reforms of the 1990s (HB 3565, Measure 50). The key features:

- **Property taxes are capped** at $5 per $1,000 of assessed value for school operations (Measure 5, 1990) and assessed value growth is capped at 3% per year (Measure 50, 1997).
- **The State School Fund "equalizes"** funding by combining state income tax revenue with local property tax revenue and distributing it on a per-pupil weighted basis (ADMw).
- **Weights** adjust funding for student characteristics: poverty, English language learners, students in foster care, students with disabilities, small school/remote factors, etc.
- The Legislature sets the total SSF amount each biennium. For the current 2025â€“27 biennium, the total fund is approximately **$10.2 billion** (as of the most recent available data).

#### Why Oregon Chronically Underfunds Education

Oregon's Quality Education Model (QEM) â€” a statutory model that estimates the cost of meeting the state's own educational goals â€” has consistently shown a **$2â€“4 billion gap** between what the Legislature funds and what is needed. Reasons include:

- Revenue competition with other state priorities (Medicaid, corrections, human services)
- Kicker law (ORS 291.349) returns surplus income tax revenue to taxpayers rather than allowing it to fund services
- No statewide sales tax, limiting the revenue base
- PERS costs consuming an increasing share of education dollars before they reach classrooms

#### Local Option Levies

Districts may ask voters to approve a **local option levy** (ORS 280.040â€“280.145) to supplement State School Fund revenue. Springfield currently has a local option levy that generates approximately **$12â€“15 million per year**. The current levy is set to expire â€” and renewal or replacement will be a critical board decision. (See the levy section below.)

### PERS: The Elephant in the Budget

The **Public Employees Retirement System (PERS)** is Oregon's defined-benefit pension system for public employees, including school district staff. It is one of the single largest cost pressures facing Springfield and every other Oregon district.

#### How PERS Affects School Budgets

- The district pays an **employer contribution rate** as a percentage of each employee's salary. This rate is set by the PERS Board every two years based on actuarial valuations.
- Springfield's blended employer rate (Tier 1/Tier 2 and OPSRP combined) is approximately **30â€“33%** of covered payroll (as of the most recent available data).
- On a payroll of roughly $85â€“90 million, that translates to **$25â€“30 million per year** in PERS contributions alone.
- Rate increases of even 1â€“2 percentage points add **$850Kâ€“$1.8M** in annual cost.

#### The Unfunded Liability

Oregon PERS carries a system-wide **unfunded actuarial liability (UAL)** that has been estimated at **$23â€“27 billion** in recent valuations. Springfield's allocated share of this liability is substantial. Districts are required to make payments toward the UAL as part of their employer rate â€” meaning a portion of every payroll dollar goes to paying down past pension promises, not funding current services.

Legislative reforms (SB 1049 in 2019) modestly reduced the rate trajectory, but PERS remains a structural cost driver that boards cannot directly control.

> âš ï¸ **Common Mistake:** New board members sometimes propose "opting out of PERS" or "switching to a 401(k)." This is not legally available to districts. PERS participation is mandated by state law (ORS Chapter 238, 238A). Advocacy for reform must occur at the state legislative level.

### The Board's Role in the Budget

The board's budget role is **governance, not management**:

- **Approve the budget** â€” The board has final adoption authority (ORS 294.456).
- **Set priorities** â€” The board should communicate its values and priorities to the superintendent *before* the budget is built, not after.
- **Appoint Budget Committee members** â€” Choose community members who bring diverse perspectives and financial literacy.
- **Fiduciary duty** â€” Board members have a legal obligation to ensure the district remains fiscally solvent. Approving an unbalanced budget or depleting reserves recklessly can expose the board to legal and political risk.
- **Communicate with the public** â€” Board members are the elected representatives of the community. During budget crises, they must be visible, honest, and accessible.

What the board should **not** do:
- Decide which specific positions to cut (that's the superintendent's job)
- Negotiate directly with unions over budget-related concessions
- Promise the public that specific programs will be saved before the budget process is complete

### The Local Option Levy

A **local option levy** is a voter-approved property tax that supplements the State School Fund. Key details:

- Authorized under **ORS 280.040â€“280.145**
- Limited to **$1.50 per $1,000 of assessed value** for school districts
- Requires simple majority voter approval at a regular or special election
- Typically authorized for **5 years**
- Springfield's current levy generates approximately **$12â€“15 million annually** (as of the most recent available data)

If the levy expires without renewal, the district would face an **additional** $12â€“15 million shortfall on top of the projected deficit â€” a catastrophic scenario. Levy renewal is therefore not just desirable but **essential** to the district's financial viability.

Board members play a critical role in levy campaigns:
- The board can authorize placing the measure on the ballot
- Individual board members may advocate for the levy as private citizens
- **The district may not use public funds to advocate** for or against a ballot measure (ORS 260.432) â€” but it may provide factual information

> âš ï¸ **Common Mistake:** Confusing a levy with a bond. A **levy** funds operations (salaries, programs, day-to-day costs). A **bond** funds capital projects (buildings, equipment). They are separate measures with separate legal authority and separate tax rates. Board members must be precise in public communications.

### Impact on Collective Bargaining

Springfield's employees are represented by collective bargaining units â€” the Springfield Education Association (SEA) for licensed staff and OSEA/classified unions for support staff. Budget deficits interact with bargaining in several ways:

- **Cost of continuing current agreements** must be budgeted before any new proposals are considered
- **Reopener clauses** in existing contracts may allow either party to renegotiate compensation mid-contract if financial conditions change
- **Reductions in force (RIF)** are governed by contract language and Oregon law (ORS 342.934) â€” seniority, licensure, and specific procedural requirements apply
- The board should **not** negotiate directly â€” that is the superintendent's (or designee's) role under ORS 243.650â€“243.782 (Public Employee Collective Bargaining Act)
- The board must **ratify** any tentative agreement reached at the bargaining table

Budget crises often coincide with difficult bargaining. Board members should understand that employee groups will advocate strongly for their members â€” that is their legal right. The board's job is to balance employee needs with fiscal sustainability and student outcomes.

### Community Engagement

Communicating a $10.4 million deficit to the community is one of the hardest tasks a board will face. Best practices:

- **Be transparent early.** Share budget projections as soon as they are available â€” don't wait until cuts are proposed.
- **Use plain language.** Avoid jargon. Say "we need to cut the equivalent of 90 teaching positions" rather than "we must reduce FTE by 90."
- **Hold multiple public forums** in different locations and at different times to maximize access.
- **Provide written materials** (fact sheets, FAQ documents, budget summaries) in English and Spanish.
- **Use the district website and social media** to share information and answer questions.
- **Listen.** Community engagement is not just about telling â€” it's about hearing what the community values most.
- **Acknowledge the pain.** Don't sugarcoat the choices. People respect honesty.

> ðŸ’¡ **Tip:** The best-run budget processes in Oregon share a common feature: the superintendent and board communicate the same message. Mixed signals â€” where one board member says "everything's fine" while another says "the sky is falling" â€” destroy public trust. Align your messaging before going public.

### What Other Oregon Districts Have Done

Springfield is not alone. Several comparable Oregon districts have faced similar structural deficits:

- **Eugene 4J:** Faced a $12 million deficit in 2023â€“24. Addressed it through a combination of staffing reductions (~70 FTE), program consolidation, school closures/consolidations, and a successful local option levy. The school closure process was politically painful and took two years.
- **Salem-Keizer:** The state's second-largest district has dealt with chronic budget shortfalls, using a combination of RIFs, administrative reductions, and state emergency funding. Successfully passed a levy after a prior failure.
- **Bend-La Pine:** Rapid enrollment growth followed by a plateau created budget stress. Addressed through careful enrollment projection, boundary adjustments, and levy funding.

Common lessons:
1. **Early and honest communication** with the community is essential.
2. **Multi-year planning** (not just one-year fixes) builds credibility.
3. **Levy campaigns** require sustained community engagement â€” starting 12â€“18 months before the election.
4. **School closures**, if needed, should be approached through a transparent, criteria-based process.

### Board Member Responsibilities During Budget Crises

As a board member during a deficit, your responsibilities include:

1. **Fulfill your fiduciary duty** â€” Ensure the district does not adopt a budget it cannot fund. An unbalanced budget violates Oregon Local Budget Law.
2. **Set clear priorities** â€” Tell the superintendent what you value (e.g., "protect classroom instruction," "maintain equity commitments," "preserve CTE") and let the administration build a budget that reflects those priorities.
3. **Avoid micromanagement** â€” Do not dictate specific cuts. The board sets policy and direction; the superintendent manages operations.
4. **Communicate with constituents** â€” Be accessible, honest, and consistent. Attend public forums. Return emails and phone calls.
5. **Support the superintendent** â€” Budget crises are stressful for everyone. The board should provide clear direction and political cover, not second-guess every decision publicly.
6. **Maintain board unity** â€” A divided board during a budget crisis invites chaos. Disagree in the boardroom, but support the final decision publicly.
7. **Think long-term** â€” Resist the temptation to use one-time fixes (reserves, grants) to avoid structural changes. A sustainable budget requires structural solutions.
